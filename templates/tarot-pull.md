## I. Premise

A small page is shown to the visitor at the moment of arrival. A single card lies face down on a parchment field, slightly weighted toward the lower half of the page. Above the card sits a quiet prompt, one line in italic, asking what is brought to the table tonight. Below the card sits one button. The whole page does one thing. It draws a card. It explains the card in plain language. It writes a short line of the visitor's choosing into a small notebook that the page itself remembers across visits. There is no progression past this single act. There is no account, no synchronization, no second view that splits attention. The page is a small ritual object that opens to the same posture every time.

## II. Surface

The page is set on a parchment color, slightly warmer in the upper half and slightly cooler in the lower half, so the card seems to rest on a desk rather than float in a vacuum. The gradient is rendered through a single radial in the body element, the inner stop at the parchment color and the outer stop a few percent darker. Type is set in Cormorant Garamond throughout the page, weight 400 and 500 and italic 400, loaded from Google Fonts. The body size is 18px with a line height of 1.6. The card title is 28px. The interpretation paragraphs are 17px. The small captions are 11px in Source Code Pro, the only sans presence on the page, used only for the date stamp on a saved note and the small label on the deck shuffle button. The accent color is a deep oxblood used for the active button text and for the small marker that distinguishes a reversed card. A second accent, a gilded brown, is used only for the borders of the card itself and for the small underlines under the card title. There are no other colors. There are no other typefaces. There are no icons except a small inline svg of a moth wing in the very top left of the page, eight pixels across, with no behavior.

## III. The deck

The full Rider Waite Smith deck of seventy eight cards is shipped inside the source. Each card holds a name, a suit (major, wands, cups, swords, pentacles), a number in its suit (the major arcana run zero through twenty one and the minor arcana run ace through ten with the four court cards page knight queen king), an upright meaning written in three short sentences in plain modern language, a reversed meaning in three short sentences in plain modern language, and an inline svg art panel drawn in the project at codegen time. The svg art is intentionally austere, no photographic detail. Each card is rendered through two to six simple paths and primitive shapes (circles, rectangles, polylines) in the gilded brown color on the parchment ground, with the card number written in roman numerals at the top inside the gilded border and the card name written below the art in 16px italic. The back of the card, shown when the card is face down on arrival or during a shuffle animation, shows a simple lattice pattern of intersecting curves filling the card area, all in the gilded brown at thirty percent opacity, with no card name visible. Every card is fully drawn inside the project. Nothing is loaded from any external image source.

## IV. The pull

On arrival the card sits face down. Above the card a small italic line reads what do you bring to the table tonight, and a single text input lies between the prompt and the card, no border, no background, the cursor placed inside. Typing a phrase or a question into the input is optional. Below the card one button reads draw the card. Pressing the button triggers a short ritual animation lasting around 1100 milliseconds. The card lifts vertically by 12 pixels, rotates on its y axis once through 180 degrees, and on the way back to flat reveals the face. A subtle parchment rustle is played through a webaudio noise burst with a soft lowpass filter, around 220 milliseconds of audio, faint enough to be missed in a quiet room. If audio is blocked, the visual reveal continues silently. A pull is reversed twenty percent of the time, drawn from a deterministic seeded random function so that the visitor sees the same probability distribution across visits even though the actual card varies. A reversed card renders rotated 180 degrees inside its frame, and a small oxblood dot is shown in the top left of the card frame as the only persistent marker of orientation. Pressing draw again at any point shuffles the deck and re draws, replacing the current card in place with the same animation. The deck cannot be exhausted, every pull is from the full deck of seventy eight.

## V. The reading

Below the card, once revealed, sits a block of three short paragraphs. The first paragraph is the card name in 22px italic, the card position written as either upright or reversed in 13px Source Code Pro just below, and the suit and number in the same small caption form. The second paragraph is the plain language meaning, three sentences, taken from the upright or reversed field of the card based on orientation. The third paragraph is a single italic line, the prompt the visitor entered above, repeated back to them under the heading and answered into. If the visitor did not write a prompt, the third paragraph is omitted entirely. The prompt text is never modified, never echoed back with additions, never paired with generated commentary. The page does not pretend to know more than the deck itself contains. Below the three paragraphs a small line in 11px Source Code Pro shows the date and time of the pull in iso form. To the right of that line a single text button reads save this to my notebook in oxblood italic. Pressing it opens an inline note row in a small notebook drawer that lives at the bottom of the page, with the prompt and the card name and a single line text field for one or two sentences the visitor wants to remember. The note saves to localStorage on blur or on a soft return inside the field.

## VI. The notebook

At the very bottom of the page sits a thin strip with the word notebook in 13px italic, lowercase. Tapping or clicking the strip expands it upward into a panel sized to roughly forty percent of the page height. Inside the panel a vertical list shows every saved note in reverse chronological order. Each row is two lines, the first line showing the date in 12px Source Code Pro and the card name in 14px italic separated by a small dot, and the second line showing the saved sentence in 13px regular. Tapping a row reveals the original prompt in 12px italic between the two existing lines without scrolling the rest of the list. A small text button to the right of each row reads forget in 11px Source Code Pro and removes the note after a small confirm prompt that reads remove this note from the notebook with cancel as the second choice. There is no edit. A note is either kept or forgotten. The notebook does not export. The notebook does not import. The notebook is small by design, never paginated, but the panel scrolls inside itself if the list is long.

## VII. Persistence and constraints

Every saved note lives in localStorage under the key tarot.pull.notebook.v1 in a flat array of objects. Each object holds an id (a short random hex string), a timestamp in iso form, the card key (the lowercased name with dashes for spaces, used only as a lookup against the in source deck), an orientation (upright or reversed), the visitor's original prompt as a plain string (allowed to be empty), and the visitor's saved sentence as a plain string. On boot the file is read once, parsed, validated, and rendered. If the storage is corrupt or unavailable, the page silently falls back to an in memory array and a small 11px italic line at the bottom of the notebook panel reads this notebook is not being kept on this device. If the visitor pulls a card without saving it, the card is not written anywhere, the next pull replaces it cleanly. There is no history of unsaved pulls. The seed for the deterministic randomness is a small counter stored alongside the notebook key, incremented once per pull, so that the same visitor on the same device sees a fresh card every time but the orientation probability holds across the population. The page never opens any network connection. There is no analytics, no telemetry, no font fallback that calls home. The Google Fonts links in index.html are the only external resources. The build is intended to work fully offline once the fonts have been cached.

The build is a single Vite project. The files are, in order, index.html with the Cormorant Garamond and Source Code Pro links in the head and the radial gradient declared on the body in a tiny style block, src/main.tsx as the React entry, src/App.tsx exporting one default component that holds the top level state for the current card, the current prompt, the notebook open or closed flag, and the saved notes array, src/components/Card.tsx for the card face and back with the flip animation handled in css through a class toggle on a single transform style, src/components/Reading.tsx for the three paragraph block below the card, src/components/Notebook.tsx for the bottom panel with its rows and forget buttons, src/components/PromptInput.tsx for the small italic line and input above the card, src/lib/deck.ts holding all seventy eight cards as data plus their inline svg art as functions returning React nodes, src/lib/random.ts holding a small mulberry32 deterministic generator keyed by the stored counter, src/lib/storage.ts wrapping localStorage in a try catch, src/lib/audio.ts holding a single function that produces the parchment rustle through a brief WebAudio noise burst and lowpass, tailwind.config.ts extending the palette with parchment, oxblood, gilded, and a faint dim variant for the captions, package.json with react and react dom and vite and tailwind only, README.md with two short paragraphs describing what the page is and how to run it. The stack is React 18 with TypeScript, Tailwind CSS for layout and color only, Vite as the build tool. State is plain useState and useReducer. No router, no global store, no context provider. No date library, native Date and Intl only. No animation library, plain css transitions and one keyframes block in the index stylesheet. No icon pack. No external image asset. The page is finished when a first time visitor opens it and sees a parchment field with a face down card and a single line asking what they bring tonight. They type a sentence, press draw, the card flips with a soft rustle, the Hermit appears upright, the three short sentences read clearly underneath, the date stamp settles below. They press save this to my notebook, type a line, the note slides into the bottom strip. They reload the tab. The notebook is intact. The card is once again face down at the top. The page does the same thing it always does.
